## Outline for writing

### H1
Beyond Pride Month: Building Inclusive Comms Structures That Last

### Opening (2 paragraphs)
Direct answer first. Inclusive comms structures are the operating-rhythm infrastructure that produces inclusion communications 12 months a year, not the heritage-month calendar that sits on top. Four structures do the work: employee-voice surfacing, audience segmentation that respects identity without flattening it, a manager cascade for sensitive topics, and measurement that goes beyond opens. Build those four and the year-round program is a downstream effect. Skip them and the June campaign keeps carrying the entire program by itself.

Second paragraph names the seasonal-comms trap so the reader understands why structures matter more than calendars.

### H2: The seasonal-comms trap
Why visibility-moment campaigns backfire when no year-round throughline exists. The pattern: a strong June program, a strong February program, a strong March program, all running through the same generic editorial process, all measured the same way, all distributed through the same channel. The campaigns work; the program does not. Employees notice the campaign cadence faster than the silence between, and the silence is where credibility erodes. Structures fix this by making inclusion comms a regular output of the IC operating model, not a quarterly campaign sprint.

### H2: What structural inclusion comms actually means
This is an operating-rhythm question, not a calendar question. Calendar work is what you publish and when. Structural work is who feeds the input, how the audience is shaped, who carries the message in sensitive moments, and what you measure after it lands. The four structures below sit underneath the calendar and run continuously. They are what the IC team builds once and maintains, rather than rebuilding every June.

### H2: The four structures that make inclusion comms last past June

Four H3 sub-points, each named clearly and explained in roughly 80–120 words.

#### H3: Employee-voice surfacing
A standing input mechanism that brings employee voices, not just ERG approvals, into the editorial cycle. Pulse questions tied to specific populations, open-comment fields on inclusion content, ERG channels where leads can flag emerging topics before the IC team writes the brief. The structure is the input pipeline itself, not the individual surveys. Without it, the IC team writes about employees rather than with them.

#### H3: Audience segmentation that respects identity without flattening it
Segmentation infrastructure that lets the team reach a specific population without treating that population as a marketing list. The structure includes opt-in mechanics for affinity groups, a clear policy on how identity data flows from HRIS to comms tooling and what it cannot be used for, and segment-level governance owned by IC and HR jointly. Tools like Cerkl Broadcast, modern intranet platforms, and HRIS-integrated comms layers can support this; the structure is the data flow and the governance around it, not the tool.

#### H3: Manager cascade for sensitive topics
Most IC programs have a manager cascade for routine business updates. Few have one tuned for inclusion topics — bias incidents, policy changes affecting specific populations, response to external events. The structure includes a manager-facing brief template that names what to say and what not to say, a fast-path activation route when an incident lands mid-week, and a feedback loop from managers back to IC. Sensitive topics fail when they default to the all-staff email because the cascade was not built.

#### H3: Measurement that goes beyond opens
Open rates and click rates run the rest of the IC dashboard; they cannot run inclusion measurement on their own. The structure is a measurement layer that tracks reach by segment, engagement gaps between specific populations and the broader workforce, opt-in and opt-out patterns over time, and pulse-feedback signals on belonging tied to specific employee populations. The structure is the reporting cadence, not the individual report.

### H2: What to do in July
The practitioner question. After the June campaign ends, the structural work for July is concrete and small. Audit the four structures: is each one running, owned, and measurable? Pick the weakest one and build it before August. The team that does this every July is the team that runs inclusion comms as infrastructure by the following June. The team that picks up the next campaign brief instead is the team that runs the same Pride Month cycle again next year.

### Closing paragraph (forward-moving takeaway)
The campaigns get the visibility. The structures carry the program. The teams running inclusive comms well are recognizable in July, not in June, because July is when the operating model either runs on its own or stops. Build the four structures and the visible months take care of themselves.

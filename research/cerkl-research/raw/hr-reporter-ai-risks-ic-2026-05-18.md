# IC Deep Dive — HR Reporter: "Using AI for internal communications? Know the risks"

**Date:** 2026-05-18   |   **Category:** Publication (practitioner-sentiment piece on AI risks in IC)

---

## TL;DR

A December 2025 Canadian HR Reporter article by Sarah Dobson lays out an academic, research-backed case that AI in internal communications damages **trust, authenticity, and perceived sincerity** — and that IC pros should "co-pilot" AI rather than let it draft relational messages. Pair this with Simpplr's 2026 State of IC findings — **52% of IC pros use AI weekly, but 29% are anxious about job security, and more than a third say AI is the reason their org plans to reduce headcount** — and the picture is consistent: **practitioners are AI-anxious, not AI-eager**. That is a counterpositioning opening for Cerkl against a competitor set (Staffbase, Firstup, Workshop, Simpplr, Poppulo) that is uniformly leading with AI maximalism.

## What it is

A **December 8, 2025** article on Canadian HR Reporter by staff writer **Sarah Dobson**, anchored on two academic studies: Peter Cardon & Anthony Coman's *"Professionalism and Trustworthiness in AI-Assisted Workplace Writing"* (International Journal of Business Communication, 2025; n=1,100 working professionals) and Linjuan Rita Men's *"Artificial intelligence for internal communication: Strategies, challenges, and implications"* (Public Relations Review; n=20 senior comms professionals, in-depth interviews). The piece is academic in tone, light on vendor names, and reads as a sober counter-narrative to the AI-maximalist marketing dominating the IC trade press through Q1–Q2 2026.

## What it does / claims

**Thesis:** AI-assisted internal communication erodes trust and authenticity, especially in **relational messages** (recognition, congratulations, motivation, performance reviews, crisis comms). The fix is "co-piloting" — humans draft, AI polishes — not "single-prompt-to-campaign" agentic delivery.

**Specific risks enumerated (verbatim shape from the article):**

1. **Trust erosion** — Recipients perceive AI-written messages as less authentic and sincere when AI assistance is high.
2. **Authenticity loss** — "Lack of human experience and human touch in the communication."
3. **Reputational harm to the sender** — Using AI may damage supervisor credibility and perceived caring. The killer quote: *"If my manager has to use AI to write a message like this, why are they my manager at all?"*
4. **Skill atrophy** — Over-reliance on AI tools without the expected time savings.
5. **Wrong tool for high-stakes scenarios** — Performance reviews and crisis communications specifically called out as places AI should not lead the draft.
6. **Sincerity decline for relational content** — Congratulations, motivation, recognition — all degraded by visible AI assistance.

**Practitioner / academic quotes:**

- **Anthony Coman** (Instructional Associate Professor & Writing Coordinator, UF Warrington College of Business): *"Begin with your brain and draft with your brain first"* — for relational messages. AI tools work best for *"polish,"* not as a starting point.
- **Linjuan Rita Men** (Professor of Public Relations, University of Florida): *"Co-piloting is the best approach"* — to preserve human touch. *"Enhance the efficiency and efficacy of the work, but always have a human in the center."*

**Solution framing:** Use AI for transactional/informative content, not relational. Adopt human-centered "co-piloting." Upskill toward strategic, creative, human-experience work. Establish clear AI usage policies. Consider context before disclosing AI use.

**Vendors named:** None directly. Generic references to "Gmail's suggested replies" and "Microsoft's Outlook Copilot." This is unusual for the trade press — and load-bearing for Cerkl positioning (see below).

## The companion data — Simpplr 2026 State of IC

The parent brief flagged a "52% weekly AI usage" stat. That stat is **not** in the HR Reporter article. It's from **Simpplr's 2026 State of Internal Communications Report** (independent survey of **448 IC pros** in North America, 85% U.S. / 15% Canada, all at orgs with 500+ employees, fielded December 2025 — same month as the HR Reporter piece). The full numbers:

- **52%** of IC practitioners use AI at least a few times a week
- **53%** report a "very high or significant" impact on efficiency
- **29%** of those closest to AI adoption cite **job security anxiety**
- **>33%** said reliance on AI was the reason their org planned to **reduce headcount**

The HR Reporter article and the Simpplr stats tell the **same story from two ends**: pros use AI, pros are afraid of AI, and pros want a framing that preserves their seat at the table.

## Who it's for

- **Primary persona:** The **AI-anxious IC practitioner** — uses AI weekly, sees the efficiency, but is hearing layoff rumors and watching their org redefine "lean comms team." Reads this for vocabulary to push back on AI maximalism in their own organization.
- **Secondary persona:** The **skeptical IC director** — has a board/CHRO asking why headcount can't shrink given AI tools, and needs research-backed talking points to defend the team's relational/strategic value.
- **Tertiary persona:** The **governance-conscious enterprise IC** at a regulated org — looking for academic citations to anchor an AI policy document.

- **Strong fit when:** The reader's KPI mix includes employee trust, sentiment, leadership credibility — not just message throughput.
- **Weak fit when:** The reader is a marketer-adjacent IC who measures success in send volume, click-through, or campaign velocity. They'll dismiss this as concern-trolling.
- **HR Reporter audience caveat:** Canadian HR Reporter skews HR/L&D, not pure IC. The piece is framed for an HR audience that does some IC work — not for senior comms directors at large enterprises. Treat as **practitioner-sentiment evidence**, not as analyst-grade market data.

## How it slots into Cerkl's positioning

This is the section that earns the deep-dive. **Four findings, ordered by leverage:**

### 1. Buyers are AI-anxious, not AI-eager. That's the opening.

Every major Cerkl competitor is leading with AI maximalism in their 2026 narrative:

- **Staffbase** — "AI quality layer," Navigator (permission-aware AI search), AI-Native EXP positioning
- **Firstup** — Five-pillar agentic AI suite (Search, Content Creator, Insights, Actions, Audience Builder), March 2026
- **Workshop** — Cici agentic AI ("builds complete internal comms from a single prompt"), Feb 2026
- **Poppulo** — Create / Target / Analyze Agents, Designer editor
- **Simpplr** — Comms AI (Feb 2026), and ironically the source of the data showing pros are anxious about exactly what they're selling
- **LumApps** — repositioned as "The leading AI Employee Hub" (2026-05-13 homepage change)

The HR Reporter article + Simpplr's own data say the **buyer's gut response to all of that is anxiety, not excitement**. If "more than a third" of IC pros work at orgs planning to reduce headcount because of AI, every AI-maximalist pitch lands as a survival threat — *to the person in the meeting*. That's a counterpositioning lane no one else is in.

**The proposed Cerkl frame:**

> "We don't sell you an AI strategy. We make sure your message gets read by the right person — AI or no AI."

Or sharper, for sales enablement:

> "Foundations is free, forever, with zero AI tax. Your job is to be the human in the loop. Ours is to deliver what you write to the people who need it."

This is **consistent with the existing reframe** in `shared/competitors.md`: Cerkl is the *delivery + measurement* layer, not the *content-generation* layer. The HR Reporter / Simpplr signal lets that reframe go further — from "we don't compete on AI features" to **"we don't sell against your job."**

### 2. Foundations free-forever is the strongest pricing manifestation of this stance.

Every competitor named above bundles AI features behind a paid tier (Workshop's $3k/yr floor, Staffbase's ~$30k floor, Firstup custom-quote, Simpplr custom-quote, Poppulo enterprise quote). When the buyer's anxiety is "AI is the reason my headcount is at risk," **a free tier that doesn't push AI at all** reads as the lowest-risk product on the market. That's a Foundations marketing line worth testing:

> "Free forever. No AI tax. No headcount pitch. Just delivery that works."

This is **not** a "Cerkl is anti-AI" pitch — Omni AI exists and ships News Digests / AI Newsletters. It's a *segmentation* pitch: Foundations buyers get a tool that doesn't presume AI is the answer; Omni AI buyers get AI surfaces when they actually want them. The HR Reporter narrative is the wedge for the Foundations marketing positioning, not for the Omni AI marketing positioning.

### 3. Omni AI needs a different narrative frame in light of this signal.

Omni AI ships News Digests and AI Newsletters — both content-generation surfaces. If buyers are reading "AI for relational messages destroys trust" in HR Reporter, Cerkl's Omni AI marketing should preemptively answer: **Cerkl's AI is for the boring stuff (digest assembly, summarization, segmentation) — not for writing the CEO's heartfelt all-hands message.** That's the "co-piloting" framing the article validates. It's *also* the framing that lets Cerkl avoid the Workshop Cici / Staffbase AI Navigator / Simpplr Comms AI escalation trap, where every quarter someone ships a more aggressive agentic feature and everyone else has to match.

**Suggested Omni AI narrative line:** *"AI for the parts of internal comms that benefit from it. Human for the parts that don't. We don't draft your manager-to-employee message. We make sure the message you wrote gets read."*

## Gotchas

- **The 52% stat is not in the HR Reporter article.** It's from Simpplr, fielded the same month the article published. The parent brief conflated the two; they're complementary signals from different sources, not the same source.
- **HR Reporter audience skew.** Canadian HR Reporter is HR-led, not pure IC. The piece carries less weight with a Senior Director of IC at a Fortune 500 than it would for a People Ops / HR generalist who also runs internal comms. Cite alongside Simpplr for the IC-specific audience.
- **Academic citations are paywalled.** Both Cardon & Coman (IJBC 2025) and Men et al. (Public Relations Review) sit behind academic paywalls. Cerkl marketing can cite the abstracts and the HR Reporter summary, but a long-form Cerkular piece quoting verbatim would need a one-off academic-access purchase or library pull.
- **"Co-piloting" is already loaded vocabulary.** Microsoft owns "Copilot" as a product name. Use "human-in-the-loop" or "human-centered AI" in Cerkl marketing copy to avoid trademark adjacency and to signal independence from the Microsoft framing.
- **Simpplr is both the source of the anxiety data AND a maximalist AI vendor.** This is awkward for them. Cerkl can quote Simpplr's own data back at Simpplr's own marketing — but should do so once, sharply, not as a sustained campaign (looks petty over time).

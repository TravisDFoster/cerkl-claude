## What it does / claims

### The 4 pressure-test questions (verbatim)

1. **"Can employees navigate this newsletter without thinking too much about it?"**
2. **"Does your format help the audience find one useful thing quickly?"**
3. **"Can employees see themselves in the newsletter?"**
4. **"Are you designing your newsletter around assumptions or actual employee behavior?"**

### Case 1 — Yale (*YourYale*, Niamh Emerson, editor)
- Emerson audited reader behavior and found employees engage *selectively*, not top-to-bottom.
- Response: enforce a predictable slot-based structure — *"We know exactly what's going in the lead slot, here's the two secondaries and here's the engagement piece."*
- Money quote on the operating principle: ***"It works because it is the same thing every week."***
- Implication: consistency-of-format is itself the navigation aid. Readers learn the grid; the format becomes ambient.

### Case 2 — Roku (Katie Satterlee, internal & editorial communications specialist)
- Hard word-count limits per section; avoid text blocks; lean on imagery to anchor stories.
- Regional personalization — including subject-line variants: *"If India has some specific line items, that goes in the subject line for them."*
- Tonal personalization too — grammar variations for British employees, based on reader feedback.
- Implication: the format isn't one newsletter; it's a *template with audience-aware variants*.

### Practitioner advice the piece distills
- Track open rates, click rates, and CTRs **by section** (not just at the email level).
- Run reader surveys and focus groups — the "actual behavior, not assumptions" half of Q4.
- Use high-quality imagery to anchor each section.
- Include employee quotes and photos — the "can employees see themselves" half of Q3.
- Keep sections short and visually distinct (color, whitespace).

## Gotchas
- **No data anchors in the piece itself.** The article cites neither Korbyt nor ContactMonkey nor any benchmark — it leans entirely on two practitioner anecdotes. Strong tactically, weak as a citation source for downstream Cerkl content. *Pair it with the Korbyt and ContactMonkey numbers when reusing.*
- **Yale and Roku are large, high-resource teams.** *YourYale* has a dedicated editor; Roku has a specialist whose job title is "internal and editorial communications specialist." The tactics translate to Foundations SMBs but the *staffing* doesn't. Be honest in any Cerkular reuse: "Here's how to do this in 30 minutes a week, not with a 3-person team."
- **Devlin/Ragan multi-week thread caveat.** This piece is part of a thread (Korbyt 2026-05-14, Week-in-Comms 2026-05-15, this 2026-05-18). Worth tracking — if Devlin pivots, the thread's value to Cerkl shifts.
- **"Can employees see themselves" (Q3) is the hardest to operationalize.** Roku does it with regional subject lines and grammar variants — both segmentation moves. Foundations buyers without segmentation data can't really answer Q3, which is its own argument for Cerkl audience manager.

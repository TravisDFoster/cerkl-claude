# McKinsey "Rewired 2.0: The Talent Plan"

**Author/Publisher:** Cerkl Internal Research (deepdive) | **Published:** 2026-05-21 | **Source type:** ic-trends deepdive synthesis | **Underlying primary source:** McKinsey Organization Blog post (2026-05-04) + Rewired 2nd-edition launch wave (book, podcast, webinar, Author Talks)

## Summary
Synthesis deepdive built off [[eric-lamarre]], [[kate-smaje]], and [[rob-levin]]'s 2026-05-04 Organization Blog post launching the 2nd edition of *Rewired*. The thesis: *"At the heart of every digital transformation is a talent transformation."* The piece argues leaders systematically underweight four people-and-AI levers — N-2/N-3 leader AI muscle, talent-model redesign (the 70-30 flips), tech-talent workplace, and human-agentic collaboration. The four levers are the cleanest 2026 analyst air cover for the "AI rollouts fail on comms infrastructure, not tooling" position. The loudest practitioner critique is the hypocrisy angle — McKinsey is cutting ~10% of non-client staff while publishing a talent playbook.

## Key Takeaways
- **The headline thesis is a quotable.** *"At the heart of every digital transformation is a talent transformation."* High-utility for downstream IC writing where a non-IC audience needs analyst-grade framing.
- **Lever #4 is the comms-adjacent quote.** *"People will need to be upskilled so they can design, operate, and supervise complex workflows."* Managers shift *"from supervising tasks to orchestrating hybrid systems — guardrails, handoffs, and judgment at the edge, along with the emotional work of helping humans feel safe alongside machine colleagues."* This is the closest McKinsey gets to naming change-comms as a discrete lever.
- **The 70-30 flips are the operational center.** **>70% of tech talent in-house** (vs outsourced); **>70% of the IT cohort as doer-engineers** (vs managers). Workforce planning now budgets *three classes of capacity* — people, agents, and (where relevant) physical automation.
- **The hypocrisy critique is the loudest practitioner reaction.** McKinsey's own AI-driven layoffs (~5,000 roles, ~10% non-client staff since 2023) frame the book as *"a productivity story for partners, and a headcount story for everyone below them."* Anyone citing the work to a sophisticated buyer needs to acknowledge this.
- **The 20% EBITDA-lift claim is the headline number with the weakest external verification.** Drawn from McKinsey's own case-collection methodology. Defensible at analyst grade, less so under independent statistical scrutiny.
- **Adjacent counter-frames exist but no direct rebuttals.** [[deloitte]]'s 2026 Human Capital Trends ("65% say culture must change significantly because of AI") is the de-facto counter-narrative — softer, EX-led, adaptability-anchored.

## Notable Claims
- McKinsey's framing: *"At the heart of every digital transformation is a talent transformation."* ([blog post](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/people-and-organizational-performance/our-insights/the-organization-blog/the-rewired-2-point-0-talent-plan))
- **>70% of tech talent in-house** (vs outsourced) and **>70% of the IT cohort as doer-engineers** (vs managers) — the two 70-30 flips. (Same source.)
- Workforce planning must budget **three classes of capacity** — people, agents, and physical automation — each with different reliability and governance profiles. ([AI Transformation Manifesto, 2026-04-07](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/tech-and-ai/our-insights/the-ai-transformation-manifesto))
- McKinsey claims AI-rewired companies lift **EBITDA by ~20%** (drawn from McKinsey case collection; methodology-dependent).
- Rewired 1.0 (2023) had 6 capabilities; 2.0 adds 30+ new case studies, the 70-30 flips, the agentic workforce-design chapter, and the human-agentic collaboration material.
- BCG (Julia Dhar et al., 2026): *"AI Transformation Is a Workforce Transformation"* — agrees with talent-centrality but goes deeper than comms via behavioral-design. ([BCG](https://www.bcg.com/publications/2026/ai-transformation-is-a-workforce-transformation))
- Bain (2026): when AI is the stated rationale for change, employee understanding of the reorg drops by **≥10 points** vs other transformations. ([Bain](https://www.bain.com/insights/want-more-out-of-your-ai-investments-think-people-first/))
- Deloitte 2026 Global Human Capital Trends: 65% say culture must shift; 85% say adaptability is critical, only 7% leading on it; only 6% making progress on human-AI work design. ([Deloitte](https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/insights/topics/talent/human-capital-trends.html))
- Gartner (2026-05-13): 50% of enterprises without a people-centric AI strategy will lose top AI talent by 2027 — sharpest convergence with McKinsey's "comms is underweighted" claim.
- McKinsey's own layoffs (~5,000 roles, ~10% of non-client staff since 2023) are the loudest practitioner critique. ([theHRDigest](https://www.thehrdigest.com/mckinsey-job-cuts-predicted-for-2026-with-ai-ambitions-to-credit-for-the-change/))

## Contradicts
- **Tooling-first AI transformation narratives** — Rewired 2.0 explicitly argues impact comes from talent, not tools. Reinforces convergent analyst position (Gartner, Forrester, BCG, MIT Sloan).
- **The book's own credibility under McKinsey's layoff context.** The text says invest in talent; the firm is cutting talent. Critics argue this materially weakens the citation utility for some audiences.

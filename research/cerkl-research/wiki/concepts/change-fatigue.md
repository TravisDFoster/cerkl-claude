# Change Fatigue

## Definition

Change fatigue is the cumulative psychological cost employees pay across sustained organizational change cycles — manifesting as reduced engagement, burnout, reduced trust in leadership, and reduced ability to absorb additional change. The frame is the dominant IC-industry counter-position to [[qualtrics]]' 2026 "more change = more engaged" claim.

## Why It Matters

Change fatigue is the IC-trade-press consensus heading into 2026. [[gallagher]]'s State of the Sector 2026 ranks it as the **#2 IC barrier** (first appearance on the top-five list). [[perceptyx]]'s 20M-response dataset names change-management effectiveness + leadership credibility as the new top engagement drivers, displacing belonging. The Qualtrics finding lands into a discourse already organized around change fatigue as the problem, not change avoidance as the solution.

## How It Works

The fatigue frame has multiple mechanisms across the body of research:

- **Volume-driven fatigue** (Gallagher): over-communicating increases distrust by 30% and burnout by 24% — too much *signal* about change is itself fatiguing.
- **Credibility-driven fatigue** (Perceptyx): perceptions of how change is handled have declined for two consecutive years; trust in senior leadership is the silent variable.
- **Saturation-driven fatigue** ([[prosci]]): above the cumulative-events threshold, engagement and capacity invert ([[change-saturation]]).
- **Manager-collapse-driven fatigue** ([[gallup]]): manager engagement collapsed 27% → 22% YoY; without intact manager capacity, the cascade fails and fatigue accelerates downstream.

## Tensions / Criticisms

- **The headline "fatigue is everywhere" hides directional nuance.** The Qualtrics data shows engagement *rises* with investment-coded change — fatigue applies cleanly to disruptive change, less so to forward-looking change.
- **Selection bias risk.** Survey populations skew toward employees still in their roles; constrained mobility (per [[abhijit-bhaduri]]) may make scored fatigue look lower than the psychological reality.
- **The "engagement-as-survey-score" critique applies to both sides.** Both fatigue research and the Qualtrics counter-finding depend on the same composite engagement measurement, which neither side has independently validated.

## Open Questions

- Is there a longitudinal study that tracks the same employees across a full multi-year change cycle and resolves the Qualtrics-vs-Gallagher disagreement directionally?
- Does the manager-cascade collapse account for most or just some of the fatigue effect?

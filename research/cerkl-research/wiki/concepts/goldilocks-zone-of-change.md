# Goldilocks Zone of Change

## Definition

The Goldilocks Zone of Change is [[benjamin-granger]]'s (Qualtrics) framing for the conditional relationship between organizational change and employee engagement: enough change to keep work *engaging* and *interesting*, but not so much that employees are stressed, burned out, or alienated. The frame anchors the 2026 [[qualtrics]] finding that more change can lift engagement — but only within a band, and only for investment-coded change.

## Why It Matters

The frame is the single biggest 2026 EX-research challenge to the prevailing [[change-fatigue]] thesis. If engagement and change are non-monotonically related — rising up to a threshold, falling beyond it — then IC practice has to track *cumulative* change load and *type* simultaneously, not just message volume or comms cadence. The Goldilocks framing makes the relationship *conditional*, which is both its strength (more honest than either "change fatigue" or "change drives engagement") and its weakness (no published quantitative threshold).

## How It Works

Three conditions must hold for additional change to *raise* engagement:

1. **Change is investment-coded** — new tech, new policies, workspace changes, strategic shifts (engagement 76-78% per Qualtrics 2026). For disruptive change (leadership change, manager change, reorg, layoffs) the relationship inverts (engagement 61-67%, gap −7pt for layoffs).
2. **Listening cadence is steady** — orgs that increased listening saw 87% engagement vs 44% for orgs that didn't ([[listening-cadence]] is Qualtrics' named moderator).
3. **Cumulative change stays below saturation** — [[prosci]]'s saturation model says additional change above the threshold inverts the relationship sharply ([[change-saturation]]).

## Tensions / Criticisms

- **No published threshold.** The Goldilocks framing is directional, not quantitative. Critics argue this makes it un-falsifiable.
- **The headline is being cited without the conditions.** Trade press (Vision Monday, TechIntelPro, Agile Brand Guide) ran the +10/-7 split largely uncritically. The framing only holds for investment-coded change paired with elevated listening — a caveat that easily drops.
- **Methodology gap.** Self-reported significant change + composite engagement index doesn't isolate which moderator (listening cadence vs. comms quality vs. manager capacity vs. leadership credibility) drives the lift.
- **Engagement may be lying.** [[abhijit-bhaduri]] argues persistently low turnover in tight labor markets makes engagement scores misleading — high scores may reflect constrained mobility, not psychological investment.

## Open Questions

- Where exactly is the saturation threshold? Prosci has the model but not a published quantitative anchor.
- Does the relationship hold longitudinally — i.e., does the same employee's engagement track this curve across a full change cycle, or is the Qualtrics finding cross-sectional only?

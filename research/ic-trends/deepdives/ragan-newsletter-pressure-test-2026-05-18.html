The 4 pressure-test questions (verbatim)

Q1

Navigate

"Can employees navigate this newsletter without thinking too much about it?"

Q2

Find one thing

"Does your format help the audience find one useful thing quickly?"

Q3

See themselves

"Can employees see themselves in the newsletter?"

Q4

Behavior over assumption

"Are you designing your newsletter around assumptions or actual employee behavior?"

Case 1 — Yale (YourYale, Niamh Emerson, editor)

Emerson audited reader behavior and found employees engage selectively, not top-to-bottom. Her response: enforce a predictable slot-based structure.

"

"We know exactly what's going in the lead slot, here's the two secondaries and here's the engagement piece."

— Niamh Emerson, editor of YourYale, Yale University · Ragan
"

"It works because it is the same thing every week."

— Niamh Emerson, on the operating principle · Ragan

Implication: consistency-of-format is itself the navigation aid. Readers learn the grid; the format becomes ambient.

Case 2 — Roku (Katie Satterlee, internal & editorial communications specialist)

Hard word-count limits per section; avoid text blocks; lean on imagery to anchor stories. The team personalizes regionally — including subject-line variants and tonal/grammar variations based on reader feedback.

"

"If India has some specific line items, that goes in the subject line for them."

— Katie Satterlee, Roku, on regional subject-line personalization · Ragan

Implication: the format isn't one newsletter; it's a template with audience-aware variants.

Practitioner advice the piece distills

  • Track open rates, click rates, and CTRs by section (not just at the email level)
  • Run reader surveys and focus groups — the "actual behavior, not assumptions" half of Q4
  • Use high-quality imagery to anchor each section
  • Include employee quotes and photos — the "can employees see themselves" half of Q3
  • Keep sections short and visually distinct (color, whitespace)

05Gotchas

The article cites neither Korbyt nor ContactMonkey nor any benchmark — it leans entirely on two practitioner anecdotes. Strong tactically, weak as a citation source for downstream Cerkl content. Pair it with the Korbyt and ContactMonkey numbers when reusing.

YourYale has a dedicated editor; Roku has a specialist whose job title is "internal and editorial communications specialist." The tactics translate to Foundations SMBs but the staffing doesn't. In any Cerkular reuse: "Here's how to do this in 30 minutes a week, not with a 3-person team."

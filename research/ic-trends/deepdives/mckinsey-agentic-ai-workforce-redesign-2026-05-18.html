McKinsey: Redesigning Technology Workforce for the Agentic AI Era

Date: 2026-05-18 Category: Analyst report (McKinsey Technology) Published: 2026-04-06 Worth watching — small move now

McKinsey's April 6, 2026 insight piece argues that as agentic AI takes on lower-level engineering work, CIOs must redesign the technology workforce across three pillars — how talent is hired, how internal capabilities are built and sustained, and how vendor relationships are structured and governed — and warns that none of it works without "top-down change management to communicate how agents can be coworkers." It is a CIO-fronted piece with a CHRO co-buyer footprint, and it explicitly names change-comms as the gate to ROI. For Cerkl, the value isn't in the framework itself but in the supporting line: agentic-AI transformation lands or fails on whether the org can communicate it — Cerkl's exact lane.

Four mounting pressures (the frame)

McKinsey opens with the four pressures that frame the rest of the piece:

  • Cut overall costs while delivering more innovation.
  • Scale global delivery while managing geopolitical risk.
  • Ensure vendor partnerships deliver ROI "rather than empty promises."
  • Hire new talent while reskilling existing workforces — "helping every employee become a technologist" — while empowering human–agent teams to deliver AI at scale.

How agents are framed

McKinsey treats agents as coworkers, not features. The phrase "how agents can be coworkers" is load-bearing — and is the same framing applied in the broader Rise of the Human-AI Workforce companion piece (which puts hybrid people–agent roles at ~one-third of the US workforce).

Three strategic choices — the CIO's seat at the table

PILLAR 1

Talent hiring

"The central question is no longer how to scale engineering capacity but how to allocate human talent where judgment and decision-making still matter."

PILLAR 2

Internal capabilities

Reskilling as a change-management problem, not a training program. "Without top-down change management to communicate how agents can be coworkers, reskilling efforts tend to remain ineffective."

PILLAR 3

Vendor relationships

Codeveloping end-to-end with cloud providers, vendors, and implementation partners — aligning on architecture, governance, and risk from the outset. Procurement reframed as capability codevelopment.

Recommendations to comms / change-management / HR leaders (inferred — piece is CIO-fronted)

Top-down change management to communicate the "agent as coworker" reframe. Shared organizational understanding of which skills matter. Modeling adoption from the top (per the companion "Redefine AI upskilling" piece — "Employees will take their cues from how leaders talk about and use AI in their own work"). Active employee participation, not passive consumption — change-management asks employees "to experiment, cocreate products, and commit to continual skill development" (per the companion "Reconfiguring work" piece).

02Who it's for

Primary audience

CIOs and CTOs at large enterprises, with the CHRO as the implicit co-owner of the talent and capability pillars. The piece is fronted to the technology buyer but the prescription requires HR and change-management partnership.

Secondary audience

VP / Head of Internal Communications at those same enterprises. McKinsey names change-comms as the make-or-break gate — which puts IC at the table whether the CIO names them or not.

Strong fit when

The org has a formal agentic-AI initiative in flight, a tech workforce undergoing visible reshape (layoffs at the junior dev end, senior AI hiring competition at the top), and a CHRO/CIO who already meet weekly.

Weak fit when

The org's AI initiative is bottom-up / shadow / "let teams experiment" — McKinsey's frame assumes top-down architectural choice-making by the CIO. Also weak for SMB and pre-agentic orgs.

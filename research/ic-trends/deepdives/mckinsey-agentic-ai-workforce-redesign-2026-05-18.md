# IC Deep Dive — McKinsey: "Redesigning Technology Workforce for the Agentic AI Era"

**Date:** 2026-05-18   |   **Category:** Analyst report (McKinsey insight piece, McKinsey Technology practice)

---

## TL;DR

McKinsey's April 6, 2026 insight piece argues that as agentic AI takes on lower-level engineering work, CIOs must redesign the technology workforce across three pillars — **how talent is hired, how internal capabilities are built and sustained, and how vendor relationships are structured and governed** — and warns that none of it works without "top-down change management to communicate how agents can be coworkers." It is a *CIO-fronted* piece with a CHRO co-buyer footprint, and it explicitly names change-comms as the gate to ROI. For Cerkl, the value isn't in the framework itself but in the supporting line: agentic-AI transformation lands or fails on whether the org can communicate it — Cerkl's exact lane.

## What it does / claims

McKinsey opens with **four mounting pressures** on technology leaders that frame the rest of the piece:

1. Cut overall costs while delivering more innovation.
2. Scale global delivery while managing geopolitical risk.
3. Ensure vendor partnerships deliver ROI "rather than empty promises."
4. Hire new talent while reskilling existing workforces — *"helping every employee become a technologist"* — while empowering **human–agent teams** to deliver AI at scale.

The article then narrows to **three strategic choices** McKinsey says are the CIO's seat at the table:

- **How technology talent is hired.** As agentic AI takes on more routine development and maintenance work, "the central question is no longer how to scale engineering capacity but how to allocate human talent where judgment and decision-making still matter." The war for senior AI talent is intensifying *while* agentic AI is simultaneously generating layoffs at the lower-skill development end.
- **How internal capabilities are built and sustained.** Reskilling the existing workforce — but explicitly *as a change-management problem, not a training program* (the McKinsey thesis across multiple 2025–2026 pieces). Quote from this piece (verbatim, via Google indexer): *"Without a shared understanding of which skills matter most — and top-down change management to communicate how agents can be coworkers — reskilling efforts tend to remain ineffective."*
- **How vendor relationships are structured and governed.** Codeveloping end-to-end with cloud providers, technology vendors, and implementation partners — aligning on architecture, governance, and risk from the outset — becomes a competitive advantage. Vendor management is reframed from procurement to capability-codevelopment.

**How agentic AI is positioned in the workforce model.** McKinsey treats agents as **coworkers, not features** — the phrase *"how agents can be coworkers"* is load-bearing. This is the same framing as the broader "Rise of the Human-AI Workforce" piece (which puts hybrid people–agent roles at ~one-third of the workforce) but applied specifically inside the technology function. Successful companies, McKinsey says, will *"infuse agentic AI throughout their organizations to create a true hybrid human-agent workforce, with effective change management to ensure people embrace this shift."*

**Recommendations to comms / change-management / HR leaders (inferred — the piece is CIO-fronted but the prescription is cross-functional):**
- Top-down change management to communicate the "agent as coworker" reframe.
- A shared organizational understanding of which skills matter.
- Modeling adoption from the top (per the companion *"Redefine AI upskilling as a change imperative"* piece — *"Employees will take their cues from how leaders talk about and use AI in their own work"*).
- Active employee participation, not passive consumption — change-management asks employees *"to experiment, cocreate products, and commit to continual skill development"* (from the companion *"Reconfiguring work"* piece).

**What the piece does *not* contain (or what we could not extract through indexers due to McKinsey's direct-fetch blocking):**
- Named author bylines (recoverable on-page; not surfaced in indexers).
- Named case studies / client examples.
- Specific quantitative claims unique to this piece — the numbers in McKinsey's 2026 agentic-AI corpus (40% US working hours automatable, ~one-third of US workforce in hybrid roles, $5-to-$1 people-to-tech investment ratio) live in the *companion* pieces ("Rise of the Human-AI Workforce," "Agents, robots, and us," "Superagency in the workplace").

## Who it's for

- **Primary audience:** **CIOs and CTOs** at large enterprises, with the **CHRO** as the implicit co-owner of pillars 1 and 2 (talent hiring + capability building). The piece is fronted to the technology buyer but the prescription requires HR and change-management partnership.
- **Secondary audience:** **VP / Head of Internal Communications** at the same enterprises — McKinsey is naming change-comms as the make-or-break gate for the agentic-AI transformation, which puts IC at the table whether the CIO names them or not.
- **Strong fit when:** The org has a formal agentic-AI initiative in flight, a tech workforce undergoing visible reshape (layoffs at the junior dev end, senior AI hiring competition at the top), and a CHRO/CIO who already meet weekly. This is the Gartner "people-centric AI" co-buyer pattern (see [gartner-people-centric-ai-2026-05-15.md](./gartner-people-centric-ai-2026-05-15.md)) viewed from the CIO side of the table.
- **Weak fit when:** The org's AI initiative is bottom-up / shadow / "let teams experiment" — McKinsey's frame assumes top-down architectural choice-making by the CIO. Also weak for SMB and pre-agentic orgs where the workforce-redesign question hasn't yet hit.

## How it slots into Cerkl's positioning

This is the load-bearing section. Two distinct angles:

### Angle 1 — Buyer-pain angle (the implicit IC role)

McKinsey is naming a **CHRO/CIO-level driver** — agentic-AI workforce redesign — that creates downstream, directly addressable demand on the IC team. The article's load-bearing claim for Cerkl: *"without top-down change management to communicate how agents can be coworkers, reskilling efforts tend to remain ineffective."* That sentence is a buyer-pain bullet for Omni AI:

- The enterprise CIO is restructuring the tech workforce around agents.
- The CHRO is co-owning the talent-and-capability piece.
- The IC team (reporting into HR or Communications, sometimes both) is being asked to translate "we're restructuring around AI" into employee-readable narrative — across email + Teams + Slack + intranet, with measurable read-through, segmented to the *specific* employee populations affected (technologists vs. business users, kept vs. reskilled, senior vs. junior).

That is the **Omni AI** job, not the Foundations job. The buyer is the IC Director who got handed an agentic-AI change-comms mandate this quarter; the budget conversation is upstream with the CHRO and the CIO.

**Sales-enablement reframe to AEs:** When you hear "we're rolling out [Copilot / Cursor / Devin / an agentic platform] across engineering this year," that is the McKinsey-named trigger. Ask: *"Who's running the comms for that — and how do you know it's landing with the engineers, the BAs, and the ops folks who all need different messages?"* That discovery question is now backed by McKinsey, Gartner ([gartner-people-centric-ai-2026-05-15.md](./gartner-people-centric-ai-2026-05-15.md)), and Forrester ([forrester-ai-rewrite-ex-deep-listening-2026-05-15.md](./forrester-ai-rewrite-ex-deep-listening-2026-05-15.md)).

### Angle 2 — Counterposition angle (the agentic-IC platform race)

Every IC-platform competitor is currently selling an *agentic IC suite*:

- **Firstup** — five-pillar agentic suite (orchestration, creation, distribution, measurement, governance)
- **Staffbase** — Navigator (permission-aware AI search) + agentic content workflows
- **Simpplr** — Comms AI (Feb 2026), orchestration-inside-the-Simpplr-workspace
- **Workshop** — Cici (AI assistant for IC writing/translation)
- **Poppulo** — Create / Target / Analyze agents

McKinsey's framing is **subtly disruptive to all of them** — and Travis should be clear about why.

McKinsey says agents are **coworkers**, not features. The vendor pitch *"we have agentic AI in our IC platform"* is the wrong unit of analysis if McKinsey's frame becomes the buying lens. A CHRO/CIO reading this McKinsey piece doesn't want a platform with agents *embedded*; they want their workforce — including the IC team — to *operate alongside* agents that may live anywhere (Copilot, ChatGPT Enterprise, custom agents in Azure AI Foundry, etc.). The platform-as-agent-container pitch becomes legacy framing.

**Cerkl's counterposition under this frame:**

Cerkl's "delivery + measurement layer" thesis fits cleanly with the McKinsey frame in a way that the platform-as-agent-container thesis does not. Cerkl can ride the wave by being the **measurement of how the AI-narrative comms actually land** — not the *source* of agentic capability, but the *substrate* that proves the change-comms are reaching the right employee on the right channel and being read. That maps onto:

- McKinsey's "without top-down change management… reskilling efforts tend to remain ineffective" → Cerkl is the proof-of-delivery layer for that change management.
- Gartner's "True ROI Index" / depth-of-AI-use metric ([Gartner 2026-05-13](https://www.gartner.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2026-05-13-gartner-predicts-by-2027-50-percent-of-enterprises-without-a-people-centric-ai-strategy-will-lose-their-top-ai-talent)) → Cerkl's analytics surface is the closest IC-vendor analog.

**The risk:** if Firstup or Staffbase repositions ahead of Cerkl as the *"agentic-comms-coworker"* platform (rather than the *"agentic-features-inside-a-platform"* platform), they capture the McKinsey frame first. Watch for that pivot in next two quarters of competitor messaging.

## Comparison to alternatives — "Redesigning Technology Workforce" vs. "Rise of the Human–AI Workforce"

Both pieces are in McKinsey's April 2026 agentic-AI corpus. They are **complementary, not redundant**. The honest contrast:

| Dimension | Redesigning Technology Workforce (2026-04-06) | Rise of the Human–AI Workforce (2026-04-30) |
|---|---|---|
| **Practice** | McKinsey Technology | McKinsey Global Institute + People & Org Performance |
| **Audience** | CIO / CTO (with CHRO co-buyer) | CHRO / CEO / Workforce strategists |
| **Scope** | **The technology function specifically** — engineering, IT, vendor management | **The entire US workforce** — all occupations, all sectors |
| **Unit of analysis** | Three pillars: talent / capabilities / vendors | Seven occupational archetypes (people-centric, people–agent, agent-centric, people–robot, robot-centric, people–agent–robot, agent–robot–centric) |
| **Headline framing** | "How CIOs rebalance hiring, reskilling, and vendor management for the agentic era" | "About one-third of the US workforce is in hybrid people–agent or people–robot roles" |

**The honest answer to "is this piece additive vs. Rise of the Human-AI Workforce?":** Yes, but narrowly. *Rise of the Human–AI Workforce* is the macro trend piece — the one to cite when arguing the agentic-workforce shift is real and broad. *Redesigning Technology Workforce* is the operational/prescriptive piece scoped to the technology function — the one to cite when arguing that **change-comms is the gate** and that the CIO can't deliver agentic ROI without the IC team. For Cerkl's sales/marketing purposes, the *Redesigning* piece is the better citation because it explicitly names change-management-comms as the failure mode; *Rise of the Human-AI Workforce* doesn't name comms at all.
